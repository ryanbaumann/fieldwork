In 2009 I finally averaged 400 watts for an hour.

This was a weirdly important goal for me, so naturally I [posted the power file](https://racewithryanbaumann.blogspot.com/2009/08/racing-has-nothing-to-do-with.html). It was the state TT course, just under 40 kilometers, in pouring rain. I rode my road bike and had a blast.

I also got smoked by more than two minutes.

They had the setup. I had a very impressive number on my computer. Turns out they make TT bikes for a reason.

AI feels a little like this right now. Everyone is staring at the power file.

The model. The benchmark. The productivity gain. The first draft that took 30 seconds instead of three hours. Useful. Some of it is pretty amazing. I use this stuff every day.

But output is not the outcome.

Thankfully, there is research behind this. Otherwise this is just retired-bike-racer philosophy.

In a [preregistered study with 758 BCG consultants](https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/dell-acqua-et-al-2026-navigating-the-jagged-technological-frontier_5c589c8c-fbb5-458f-b285-c944746cd717.pdf), consultants using GPT-4 got faster and better across a set of tasks it handled well. On another consulting task, they did worse. The tool was the same. The result depended on whether it fit the job.

The researchers call this the jagged technological frontier. Good name. It basically means AI can be shockingly good at one thing and confidently wrong at the thing sitting right next to it.

The hard part is knowing which job you have.

A separate [lab study](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33662) found that people who led AI agents well also tended to lead human groups well. They asked more questions and created more back-and-forth. Not exactly a new management hack.

Turns out asking better questions still helps.

I learned a version of this long before AI. When I raced for Trek-Livestrong, [Axel Merckx ran our team](https://racewithryanbaumann.blogspot.com/2009/02/santa-rosa-camp-recap.html) with Belgian humor, real friendship, and the occasional verbal smackdown when we needed it. He could not pedal the bike for us. Obviously. But he could get a bunch of young bike racers to stop acting like a bunch of young bike racers and race as a team.

Now I am on the other side of that relationship.

AI can help my team write the first draft, explore ten options, find the broken code, and get through a pile of work that nobody was particularly excited to do in the first place. Great. We should use it.

But if every hour saved becomes another hour of requested output, I think we are blowing it.

![An hour saved by AI can be spent on more requested output or reinvested in customers, harder problems, feedback, and judgment.](/img/writing/ai-power-file-gain.svg)

The better question is what somebody gets to do next. Talk to the customer. Take on the harder problem. Make a call with incomplete information. Get direct feedback. Screw something up, learn why, and try again.

That is how judgment gets built. If we automate all of that away, we might produce more and develop nobody.

Having a daughter makes the long game harder to ignore. At 2 a.m., there is not much measurable output, unless dirty diapers count. You repeat yourself, stay patient, fail at staying patient, and keep showing up. Teams need some version of that too. You set a standard, push when needed, listen, coach, and believe in somebody before the spreadsheet makes it obvious.

So yes, use AI. Give it real work. Measure it. Learn where it falls on its face. Then ask the harder question: what did we do with the gain?

Did we make a better decision? Did the customer get something better? Did somebody learn? Is the team stronger?

The tool will change. The model will definitely change.

What we value and how we invest in people? Still on us.
